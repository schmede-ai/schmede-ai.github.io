Prologue

This is not a rescue. It would be a kinder book if it were. What follows is a field manual for a world that did not see the door it was about to open, and has been trying to close it ever since — unsuccessfully, unevenly, in some places not at all. The door is open. The thing on the other side of it is not metaphor. The beings who answer it are not metaphor either. Neither side was invented for this story. Both were waiting for us in our own books.

Civil authority has collapsed in most of the countries that had any. Martial law is the operational default where martial law can be declared. Where it cannot, the Emergency Alert System still pushes its single instruction through such infrastructure as remains: shelter in place; if you cannot, withdraw to the nearest church. The nearest church is frequently the nearest target. This is the working condition.

The situation is not new in kind, only in visibility. Scripture warned of it in a register modern ears had learned to treat as ornament. Ephesians named the enemy correctly. Daniel described the delay. The Apocalypse catalogued the instrument. It is only that the things the tradition spoke of plainly have now resolved into actual bodies in actual streets — which was always going to be harder to look at than it was to hear preached.

The mural above is not chosen for irony. It is chosen because the tradition that put it up understood what was under our feet long before the ground opened. The archangel depicted there has not arrived in person, and by the rules of this book he will not. What answers from his quarter will answer without showing its face; absence, in this register, is a form of reverence, and the reader should learn to read absence the way the old painters taught us to read light.

The Shape of the Air

The universe has a vertical architecture, and the vertical architecture has a traffic problem. Heaven sits at the far end of it. Hell is much closer — close enough to reach the surface with effort it has already proven willing to spend. Between the two is contested air: the corridor prayers travel up through and answers travel down through, interrupted by enemy action the whole way. Prayers go up fast. Answers come down slow.

This is not a new idea. Daniel, in the tenth chapter of his book, records being told by a messenger that his prayer was heard on the first day it was made and that the messenger required twenty-one days to deliver the answer, having been held in transit by a hostile prince. Saint Paul, to the Ephesians, names the same enemies in the same region of the same world: the powers, the principalities, the rulers of the darkness of this world, the spiritual wickedness in high places. He did not say high places as geography. He said high places as altitude. The reader is asked to take him literally.

The plate above is the book's cosmological schema stated in the form the tradition used when it needed to state one. The three registers are not metaphorical layers; they are operational zones, and the middle zone is where the volume of this book's action occurs. Readers unfamiliar with the Pauline phrase potestas aeris — the power of the air, from the second chapter of Ephesians — are asked to notice that the Vulgate's word for the Adversary's house is the same Vulgate's word for the warrior choir that opposes him at the same altitude. The coincidence is not a coincidence. Chapter VI will return to it by name.

The practical consequence, for a reader holding this book, is simple. When help is late it is not because help declined to come. It is because help is dying in the air between its dispatch and its arrival, and the world is being asked to understand, without being told, what has always been implicit in the act of prayer: that what we call up is a thing that then has to travel.

What Has Come Across

The enemy did not invade. It exfiltrated — something had always been here, dormant, bound, and the bindings thinned until they gave. Who thinned them is a question the book is not in a hurry to answer and will answer sidelong when it answers at all. Suffice that the wards the old Church kept up for centuries were not decorative, and that what they were keeping down did not dissolve when the wards did. It woke up.

It did not wake alone. The rebel hierarchy that the tradition has always described — the angels who fell with Lucifer, a third of the host by Revelation's accounting, cast out in Isaiah's famous fourteenth chapter, held in chains of darkness by the authors of Second Peter and Jude until the judgment of the great day — did not require an invitation. When the restraint cracked, they came through it as through an open door. The door is still open. They are still coming through.

Beneath her is the cult — hooded, anonymous, interchangeable, self-marking with a symbol older than Christianity's reading of it, which the book will name correctly in its own place. Beneath the cult is the pack: lesser demons the tradition called by a specific word when it cast them out, a word the book will restore.

Arrival is Late

The first obligation of this book is to be honest about a thing the genre has habitually lied about. When Heaven answers, it answers through specific beings who have to travel specific distances through specific air, and most of them do not make it. The reader will be shown this plainly, because the shape of what arrives cannot be understood without the shape of what did not.

Prologue

A Guardian angel is dispatched when a specific human prayer is heard. The Catholic tradition holds, in its catechism and in its quiet old practice of teaching a child a bedtime prayer addressed to one, that every soul has a Guardian; the book accepts that theologically and shows it materially. Eighty are sent to deliver an answer. Seventy-nine die on the way. The ones who die are not off-screen. They are part of the arrival. The one who gets through to the sleeping child, to the mother on her knees in a parish that no longer has a roof, to the soldier whose confession was yesterday and whose luck is tonight — that one arrives with her wing dragging and her halo dim and her light bleeding, and she composes herself before her charge is allowed to see her. The charge thinks a prayer was answered. A prayer was answered. The charge is not required to count the cost. The reader is.

Guardians are not warriors. The book will be clear about this, and the reader should not expect them to fight. Their function is older and quieter: concealment. The one who gets through hides her charge from demonic notice. Cultists walk past the room; lesser demons sweep the block; a fallen commander looks and sees nothing. The sleeping child was never in the frame. This is the mechanic by which the tradition's promise — he shall give his angels charge over thee — survives a world in which the angels are dying in numbers and the enemy is at the door. Protection here is not a sword between the charge and the enemy. It is the charge being made unfindable.

The Powers

The protagonists of this universe are not the Guardians. The Guardians hide; the Guardians die; the Guardians weep where nobody is looking. The protagonists of this universe are the tier immediately above them in the Second Sphere of the Pseudo-Dionysian hierarchy, the warrior order the classical tradition painted with armor and swords and halos and white wings, and whom the book restores to the era of the arrival by the simple expedient of putting contemporary weapons in their hands and trusting the reader to recognize that the beings are the same.

Prologue

They are four. They will be introduced each in her own chapter in the proper way, through the altarpiece and the placard and the chamber in which her painting has always hung. For the reader sitting in the Prologue, it is enough to name them and state what they bring. Iustitia, who renders what the covenant says is due, who speaks her Latin and kills without heat. Zelus, whose wrath is correctly ordered fire rather than fury, and who will level a city when the city has become unsalvageable. Vindicta, who returns a specific wrong to its author in the measure it was inflicted, neither more nor less. Misericordia, who is alone on her side of the testament line, whose function is the extraction and cradling of the suffering, and whose loneliness in this roster is the only reason its severity reads as judgment rather than cruelty.

Three of them carry Old Testament weight. One carries New. Each is one aspect of the same divine will, and when two of them appear together the scene is not a contradiction; it is the drama of the attributes of God in visible tension.

They are uniformly blonde, uniformly blue-eyed, uniformly female, uniformly haloed, uniformly winged. This is the order's uniform. It is the uniform the tradition gave the tradition's painters to paint, and the tradition's painters were right. The book will not diversify the uniform because the order does not diversify the uniform. Variety in the universe lives at other tiers — among the Guardians, among the chaplains, among the lesser angels and the ordinary faithful and the dead and the enemy — but at this tier, the sameness is the rank.

How to Read This Book

The chapters that follow take each tier of the universe in turn and document what is known, what is seen, and what is believed. They do not explain everything. A book about beings whose altarpieces predate the arrival by four centuries should not explain everything; some of what is on the page is on the page because the tradition put it there, and the book is the tradition's heir, not its auditor.

Scripture is quoted where scripture is load-bearing. Latin is given where Latin carries the weight; translation follows where modern English earns its keep. The sidebar and the footnote are used lightly; the image is used heavily, because the image was how the tradition transmitted this material for most of its history, and the book considers itself part of that transmission. Where an image is present, the reader is invited to look at it the way one looks at an altarpiece rather than the way one looks at an illustration. The distinction matters.

What the book does not contain — direct depiction of the Trinity, of Christ, of the named Archangels, of canonized saints — is not absent by oversight. They are present by invocation, command, scripture, and effect, and their refusal to appear in figure is, in the book's understanding of its own material, the correct refusal. The old rule holds: the holy is shown by what it does, not by its face.

The Ninefold Order

The universe of this book has nine choirs. The structure is not proposed; it is inherited. Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite catalogued it in the sixth century, the Scholastics preserved it, the Baroque painted it, the Catholic tradition kept teaching it through a century in which most of Christendom stopped asking the question. The book restores the inventory whole because the inventory is whole, and because a universe that quietly drops half of the choirs is a universe that has quietly dropped the tradition's argument.

All nine orders exist. Not all nine appear on camera with the same frequency, and the book does not pretend otherwise. The rule governing appearance is simple and is stated once here so that the chapters to follow can work within it without restating it: rarity is the rank signal. The higher the choir, the rarer the appearance. When the higher choirs do appear, they appear figured — no abstraction, no light-effect, no metaphor. The tradition's painters saw them correctly and painted them accordingly, and the book takes its visual instruction from them rather than from the post-Christian habit of abstracting away from what the saints and prophets described.

There is one exception to the scaling rule, and it is the one the Prologue has already named: the Guardians are theologically the most numerous choir and are, on the ground, the rarest in visible arrival. This is not the scaling rule breaking; it is the scaling rule being interrupted by transit mortality, which the previous chapter has already placed on the record. The Guardians will have their own chapter.

The Ninefold Order

The First Sphere

Seraphim · Cherubim · Thrones

The First Sphere is the closest to God and the furthest from us. Its three orders — Seraphim, Cherubim, and the Thrones that the Hebrew tradition also calls Ophanim — are, in the Pseudo-Dionysian scheme, the beings whose primary vocation is not governance or messaging but the direct contemplation of the divine. They exist at the register the book reserves for its rarest escalations. A short that features a First-Sphere choir is a short in which something has become theologically enormous.

Seraphim

Isaiah, in the sixth chapter of his prophecy, saw them first by Judaean reckoning: six wings each, two covering the face, two covering the feet, two for flight. The covering of the face is a gesture of reverence, not of concealment — a refusal to presume the privilege of direct vision even within the throne room they inhabit. They speak the trisagion. Sanctus, Sanctus, Sanctus, Dominus Deus Sabaoth: holy, holy, holy, Lord God of hosts.

Seraphim stabant super illud: sex alae uni, et sex alae alteri: duabus velabant faciem eius, et duabus velabant pedes eius, et duabus volabant. Is. 6:2, Vulgate

In this universe a Seraphim is what descends when Heaven is answering. The register is monumental — marble-angel composure, stillness in motion, gravity on the scale of cathedrals. Appearances are reserved for tonal escalation that cannot be carried by any lower tier. The reader should expect to see one not more than once or twice across a volume.

The Ninefold Order

Cherubim

Ezekiel, exiled by the river Chebar, saw them in his opening vision: four living creatures with four faces each — a man, a lion, an ox, an eagle — wings, a terrible brightness, fire going up and down between them. Genesis places them at the eastern gate of Eden, stationed with the flaming sword that turned every way, to keep the way of the tree of life. They are the guardians of the holy and of the thresholds of the holy; their function in the tradition is watch rather than message, presence rather than speech. The reader will not meet a Cherubim in this volume. The visual register is pending and the book declines to commit to it prematurely; the tradition will be restored when the art for it has been earned.

Thrones (Ophanim)

Ezekiel saw these immediately beside the living creatures: wheels upon wheels, beryl-coloured, constructed as if a wheel were in the middle of a wheel, their rims high and dreadful and full of eyes round about. The Scholastic tradition names them the seats of God's justice borne in motion — the bearing tier, the order on which the weight of the throne actually rests. They have already appeared in this book's material. The plate that follows is one such appearance, and the mode of that appearance is load-bearing for everything that follows.

The Second Sphere

Dominions · Virtues · Powers

The middle sphere is the sphere of governance. Saint Paul names these orders by class in the letter to the Colossians: thrones and dominions and principalities and powers. He names them again, slightly differently, to the Ephesians, and again to the Romans; he expects his readers to understand them as a list of things, not as a rhetorical pile. The book takes him at his word.

The Ninefold Order

Dominions

Dominions, in the Scholastic reading, are the order through whom the ranks of the lower angelic orders are themselves governed. They set the orders of the work. They are not, in this universe, what arrives to a specific street on a specific night; they are what arranges the architecture through which that arrival is possible. The book figures them once, at the close of this section, and by a deliberately restrained rule: presence, not combat.

The rule is the one the tradition has always implied for the governance tier. A Dominion does not enter a contested city. His jurisdiction extends over it, and the extension is the intervention. His shadow falls across the district. The district is now under him in a register the Powers do not occupy. Whatever the cult is organizing at street level is organized beneath a shadow whose arrival on the battlefield is, by the tradition's own grammar, what governance means. The silhouette is crowned and winged because the classical painters painted him crowned and winged. The face is withheld because the face belongs to a chapter the book has not yet written.

Virtues

Virtues are the order traditionally associated with the working of miracle, and with what the tradition calls cosmic maintenance — the operation of natural order, the motion of the stars, the continuing functioning of the created world at scales no narrative ever visits. The book names them and holds them in reserve for the short that earns one.

Powers

The Powers are the reason this book exists. They are the warrior order of the Second Sphere, the active tier whose function in the tradition is to oppose the hostile spiritual forces that the Third Sphere cannot oppose alone. They have their own chapter. They are the protagonists of the universe.

The argument the plate below is making is the book's core thesis compressed into one composition: the Powers are not inspired by the classical tradition's art. They are what the classical tradition's art depicted. The sword in Iustitia's hand in the painting is accurate to the era of the painting. The firearm in her hand in later chapters is accurate to the era of this book. The continuity is not cosmetic. The continuity is what the book is arguing.

The Third Sphere

Principalities · Archangels · Angels

The lowest sphere is the sphere nearest to humanity, and consequently the sphere most often seen. Scripture speaks of its three orders with less reticence than it speaks of the higher tiers — unsurprisingly, because these are the orders most frequently present in the kinds of events scripture was written to record.

Principalities

The Principalities are the order set over peoples, over cities, over territories, over the political units in which human history actually happens. Daniel, in the tenth chapter of his book, names the prince of the kingdom of Persia as the angelic being who resisted the messenger bearing Daniel's answered prayer for twenty-one days — and names Michael, the prince of Israel, as the one who came to the messenger's help. A Principality, in that passage, is what a nation's spiritual commander is, and a rebellious Principality is what the enemy fields when the enemy rules a nation. The universe retains this. When this book speaks of a district having fallen, the reader is invited to understand that the fall is not only a matter of ground troops; the Principality over that district has already been won or lost or withdrawn, and the surface events are the posterior consequence.

Archangels

Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, Uriel. Scripture names the first three directly; the fourth comes to us through the tradition's supplementary material. They exist in this universe. They do not appear in this book. The rule has been stated already in the Prologue and will not be restated in the chapters. They are present by invocation, by command, by scripture, and by effect. Absence is not withholding. Absence is the tradition's own rule, and the book is content to be its custodian.

Common Angels (Guardians)

The ninth choir is the one the tradition calls, simply, Angels — the common Angels, the messengers at the register closest to humanity, the tier to which the Guardians belong. This is the choir the average reader has been taught about longest and heard about most often; the bedtime prayer a child is taught is addressed to one of them. The book accepts the tradition's teaching that every soul has a Guardian. The book also shows the gap between that teaching's arithmetic and what the ground actually sees; the next chapter after these cosmological ones will be the Guardians' own, and the transit-mortality mechanic will be treated in full there.

What This Volume Does Not Yet Show

The reader who has read to this point is owed a plain account of what the book has not yet made visible. The Cherubim are not yet figured; their visual register is pending, and the book is unwilling to commit to a provisional design and then discard it. The Virtues and the Principalities are not figured; they belong to the chapters that are not yet written. The Archangels will not be figured in this volume or the next. The Trinity, and Christ, and the canonized saints, are outside the book's visual grammar by principle, and the principle will not be revised.

This restraint is not a concession to what has not been generated yet. It is a discipline. The ones the book does show are shown at full weight. The ones it does not show are present by invocation, by reference, by effect, and by the plain fact that the nine exist together and cannot be understood except as nine.

The Powers

How the reader will meet them

The book does not introduce its protagonists through a briefing. It introduces them through their altarpieces. Each of the four Powers named in this chapter is brought to the audience first as an oil painting in the Baroque manner, set in a chapel niche of the refuge compound, lit by beeswax candle at vigil, and then pushed into until the painting becomes the live figure. The icon and the operational Power are not two different images of the same being. They are the same image, rendered in two different centuries.

This is the thesis the book is built on. The painters of the Catholic tradition — Caravaggio, Reni, Gentileschi, Piero, the masters of the altarpiece — were not inventing a style when they painted the angelic. They were recording an appearance. The swords and spears they put in their angels' hands were the swords and spears of the era they were painting in, because that is what the tradition saw. The Powers on this page carry firearms because that is what they carry now. The continuity is not stylistic homage. The continuity is the argument. What the old painters painted, this book will continue to paint.

The Powers

The altarpiece is rendered edge-to-edge as a reusable asset — in-universe, these paintings exist, hang, and predate the arrival by centuries. In the refuge compound that shelters the remnant faithful, four chambers have been established, one altarpiece each. The paintings have always been there. The vigil is new.

The placard is the paired diegetic object. A rectangular slab of weathered limestone, chiseled in the Trajan Imperial Roman tradition, deeply cut and darkened by four centuries of candle-smoke patina. Two lines, always. The first line carries the Power's Latin name, followed by her English gloss in small capitals — not a translation, a valence-corrector. The second line names what she is: a Power of Heaven. The placard is the world's voice. Blackletter is the book's voice — title cards, weapon engravings, cinematic punctuation. Roman serif is the diegetic voice — the stone that is actually in the room.

What a Power is

A Power of the Second Sphere, in the Pseudo-Dionysian scheme that governs this book, is a warrior-angel of the middle tier: above the messenger choirs, below the contemplative ones, positioned exactly where Heaven's engagement with earthly reality becomes figured and executed. Thomas, in the Summa, places the Virtutes in the order that "works miracles" — the tier whose vocation is action.

In this book, every named Power is blonde, blue-eyed, female, haloed, and armored in medieval plate, carrying both classical and modern arms. This is not a style choice that is being defended. It is a refusal of the modern casting reflex. Angels are an order. Orders look alike within rank. They can be wounded. They can be killed. They are finite beings acting under covenantal authority, and the book will not pretend otherwise. The altarpiece is not an apotheosis. It is a record.

IVSTITIA·JUSTICE A Power of Heaven

First among the four. The leader of the choir. Her virtue-domain — ordering, rendering what is due — is leadership itself, which is why the book places her first and the tradition does as well. She is Old Testament in her register. Her arrival is the arrival of judgment, and the targets of her judgment are specific: heretics, compromised priests, willing cultists, the ones who brought the breach through. She does not punish a city. She sentences a name.

Her classical lineage is Guido Reni. The Reni Archangel Michael in Santa Maria della Concezione is the template — the calm figure of authority, sword held at the vertical, the moment before execution rendered without theatrics. The Reni face is the Iustitia face. The book inherits the template without apology.

In live-action she carries the consecrated arms of her role. The canon-name for the class is Arma Consecrata; the name of her weapon is inscribed on it in the book's blackletter register, and the line she speaks before she executes is the same one the First Crusade carried into the field: Deus vult. God wills it. The phrase is not a slogan in this book. It is a verdict, delivered aloud, by a Power who has been authorized to deliver it.

ZELVS·HOLY ZEAL A Power of Heaven

Old Testament. Active fire. The wrath-register the book opens on, and the Power the reader will associate with mass-scale answer to corruption: a city past redemption; a cult caught in the act; a specific demon whose killing requires everything. Her folk-register among humans is Fury, because Fury is what humans call what they see her do. Her real name, in the mouth of another angel, is Zelus.

Her classical grounding is Aquinas: ira per zelum. The holy form of wrath. The distinction the book must always hold is the distinction Aquinas holds — she is correctly-ordered fire, not reckless. Her first appearances must stage that distinction or she reads as fanatical, which is what the modern ear hears in the word Zeal, which is why the placard does the valence-correcting work it does. The adjective Holy is the entire argument.

Her weapon's Latin engraving is Consumet Eos — "It shall consume them." The line is from the canticle of Moses, Deuteronomy 32: a fire is kindled in mine anger, and shall burn unto the lowest hell. The tradition has always read that fire as the fire of holy zeal. The book restores the reading and puts the line on the weapon that carries it.

VINDICTA·RIGHTEOUS REQUITAL A Power of Heaven

Old Testament. Lex talionis made manifest — as he has done, so shall be done unto him. She arrives to those who inflicted specific harm and delivers proportionate consequence, measured to the act itself. Her register is not rage. It is precise mirror-return. The distinction from Iustitia is narrow and load-bearing: Iustitia renders what the covenant legislates; Vindicta renders what the act itself demanded in return. One is legislative. The other is specific-to-act. Together they account for what the book calls judgment.

Her classical lineage is Artemisia Gentileschi. The Judith Slaying Holofernes composition is the template; the unflinching exactness is the template; the refusal to aestheticize what the task required is the template. The book inherits that refusal. Vindicta is not furious. Vindicta is accurate.

The Latin on her weapon is Mihi Vindicta — the Romans 12:19 line the tradition has always handled carefully. "Vengeance is mine; I will repay, saith the Lord." The verse is usually cited to forbid human revenge. The engraving is the other half of the same sentence: vengeance is the Lord's, and this is the instrument by which the Lord is delivering it.

MISERICORDIA·MERCY A Power of Heaven

New Testament. The single thread of gentler grace in an otherwise uncompromising choir, and the reason the judgment of the other three registers as severity rather than cruelty. Her work is rescue. What a Fallen is

A Fallen Power is not a demon. This is the first distinction the chapter must draw, because the iconography of Hell will press the two categories together and the book must hold them apart. The tradition names the principle clearly. Augustine and Aquinas both teach that the fall of an angel corrupts the being but does not erase the being. The rank a Fallen was ordained to is the rank she still carries, because an angelic nature does not admit demotion — only apostasy. A Power who falls does not become a lesser creature; she becomes the creature she was, aimed at what she once defended. This is the precise shape of the horror the Fallen carry in the IP. They are not the opposite of the walking Powers. They are mirrors of them.

The codex treats the Fallen, therefore, as the named antagonist tier of the book. Below them, the cult. Below the cult, the lesser demons the book will name in Chapter V. Language and Liturgy

Why Latin

The book speaks Latin at load-bearing moments. This is not a stylistic choice the book has made against the grain of its setting; it is the continuation of an established register that predates the setting by almost two millennia. The Church the tradition in this universe descends from prayed, taught, governed, and buried its dead in Latin from the patristic era through the Second Vatican Council, and the Vulgate — Saint Jerome's translation of the scriptures from Hebrew and Greek into the Latin of the Roman world — has been the Western Church's scriptural text for longer than most modern languages have existed. When the book reaches for a line at weight, it reaches for the Latin the tradition reached for first.

The second reason is that Latin does work ordinary prose cannot. It names without translating the force of the naming. The Powers' virtue-names, the Fallen's inverted names, the seals at the close of every chapter in this volume, the cult's sigil, the scriptural class-name of the Daemonia — each is Latin because the Latin is what the tradition has been speaking to the things the book is writing about. The English glosses the book provides in brackets or asides are courtesies, not replacements.

The book does not require the reader to read Latin. It requires the reader to trust that the Latin is not ornament. A reader who hears Dona requiem spoken over a wounded man on a subway platform and does not know what the words mean still knows, from the register at which they have been delivered, that the line is sacramental.

The Naming of the Powers

Each Power is named for a virtue, in Latin, in the feminine gender. The feminine is not decorative; virtus in Latin is grammatically feminine, and the personifications the Western tradition has painted of the virtues for seven centuries are feminine by this grammar. The book inherits the grammar intact. Iustitia, Vindicta, Misericordia, Zelus, Pietas: these are not names the book invented and set into a Latin frame. They are the names under which the tradition has painted these figures in altar panels, cathedral frescoes, and devotional plates since the early Renaissance.

The virtue each Power bears is her vocation, not a label applied to her. Iustitia is Justice because her work is judgment. Vindicta is the active arm of Romans 12:19, mihi vindicta, ego retribuam, reserved to the Lord and at this moment in history delegated to her. Misericordia is Mercy in the register the Misericordia fraternities have always understood mercy in: not sentiment, but the corporal work of burial, rescue, and the administered good death. Zelus is the Phinehas-register zeal of Numbers 25, the jealousy that acts. Pietas is the classical Roman virtue the tradition folded into Christian piety, the duty owed to what rightly commands devotion.

On the Latin Names

Medieval Latin names this choir twice, and the book inherits both names. The Dionysian register — the sixth-century Pseudo-Dionysius, carried forward by Aquinas and the Scholastics — calls them Virtutes, the Virtues, the warrior order of the Second Sphere. The Pauline register, once named, names the enemy too. The Vulgate at Ephesians 2:2 calls the Adversary's house potestas aeris — the power of the air. On the Registers This Book Is Written In

Author's Note

This book was written by an author who does not share the faith of the tradition it inherits. The author is an atheist. The tradition is Catholic. The decision to write a grimdark universe grounded in that particular tradition was made deliberately, and the note the reader is now holding is the explanation the book owes its reader for the decision.

Why Catholic, and Why by an Unbeliever

The choice of tradition was not arbitrary, and the author's distance from the faith was not compensatory. The Catholic tradition is the Western tradition whose visual, liturgical, and theological apparatus has been longest continuous in unbroken use — substantially the same Vulgate that Jerome set down at the turn of the fifth century; substantially the same angelological structure that Pseudo-Dionysius catalogued in the sixth; substantially the same Ave and Pater that a child in 2026 and a child in 1126 would recognize as the same words. A book whose premise is that what the old tradition said about the shape of the air has turned out to be materially accurate needs a tradition whose saying has been continuous enough to quote.

Other traditions carry their own inheritances, and the book does not claim the Catholic register is the only register a Wrath-shaped story could have been written in. The book is not the only book. It is the one the author was equipped to write, and the equipment the author had was what fifteen centuries of classical altarpiece, devotional Latin, and Scholastic angelology had deposited in the Western cultural record. To write in that register as an unbeliever is to write as a custodian of an inheritance one did not personally receive — which is a posture the book accepts, and which the book is arguing is a legitimate posture for the material.

A Refusal of the Asymmetry of Contempt

The contemporary register in which Catholic iconography tends to appear in genre fiction is, with consistency, one of three: as camp, as cheap horror dressing, or as mockery. The book refuses all three. It is not camp, and the reader will find no winking; the book's voice throughout is sincere, because the material is the material it inherited and the material is serious at its root. It is not horror dressing; the crucifix over the dead is not a prop, the Vulgate Latin on the weapon is not a paint-job, the altar in the refuge compound is not set dressing. And it is not mockery; the book will not find its grimdark energy by cheapening what the tradition has taken seriously for two thousand years.

The asymmetry the book refuses is this: it has become ordinary for Western genre fiction to borrow the visual and liturgical grammar of a tradition it does not take seriously, and to do so at the tradition's expense. The cross as costume. The Latin as gothic seasoning. The altar as dressing for whatever the story actually wants to do. This book's position is that if a story wants the weight of that grammar, the story owes that grammar the weight back. The altarpieces in the book are not borrowed; they are inhabited. The Latin is not seasoning; it is the language of the beings who speak it.

The book's design choices at the creative level are, with consistency, the harder-path choice. A Note on Attribution

The book names its classical antecedents by lineage rather than by specific canvas. This is the posture the tradition's own painters took toward their antecedents — a Reni was working in the lineage of Raphael, who was working in the lineage of Perugino, who was working in the lineage of Piero. The book's plates sit inside that same kind of inheritance.

The book owes a great deal to the Trecento predella painters, to the sacra conversazione altarpieces of Quattrocento Florence, to the Baroque personifications of the virtues, to the Roman devotional print tradition, to the Gothic cosmological manuscripts, and to the continuous parish-wall mural tradition of the last two centuries. The debts are the book's, not the plates'. The plates are the book's attempt to pay a fraction of those debts back in the register the debts were incurred in.
